# Objects in the future are closer than they appear

*Quietly.*

I've been in tech long enough to know that the biggest shifts don't announce themselves. They don't arrive with a press release or a keynote. They show up disguised as toys, dismissed by the people who should be paying the most attention.

Right now, something is happening that will be obvious in five years but feels like a fringe opinion today: the era of needing a company to build something that matters is ending.

Not winding down. Ending.

---

## The coordination tax

I've spent years inside large engineering organisations — the kind with quarterly roadmaps, sprint ceremonies, platform teams, and layers of process designed to keep hundreds of people pointed in roughly the same direction. I know how these machines work. I also know how much of that machinery exists not because it's necessary, but because the tools we've had until now demanded it.

You needed fifteen people because no single person could hold the full picture. You needed a frontend team, a backend team, a data team, a design team, and a project manager whose entire job was making sure those teams actually talked to each other. The complexity wasn't in the product. It was in the coordination.

AI is dissolving that coordination tax.

Not in theory. Right now. In apartments and home offices and co-working spaces in cities that don't have venture capital ecosystems, people are building things that would have required a seed round and a twelve-person team two years ago.

One person. One laptop. Shipping product.

---

## The solopreneur ceiling just got ripped off

This isn't a new idea. The "solopreneur" narrative has been floating around for a decade. But there's a difference between what was possible in 2016 and what's possible today, and the gap is so large that using the same word for both feels misleading.

In 2016, a solo founder could build a landing page, maybe a simple app, definitely a newsletter. They were constrained at every turn — by what they could code, by what they could design, by what they could afford to outsource. The ceiling was low. You could survive alone, but you couldn't compete alone.

In 2026, a solo founder can architect a full platform, generate production-quality code, design interfaces, write copy, analyse data, handle customer support, and iterate faster than a funded team stuck in consensus-driven decision-making. The ceiling hasn't been raised. It's been removed.

The constraint is no longer capability. It's imagination.

If you spend any time on X or Indie Hackers, you're already seeing this. Solo builders hitting $10K, $50K, $100K MRR with no team, no funding, no permission. Competing with products built by companies with fifty engineers and Series B backing. This used to be a once-a-year anomaly. Now it's a weekly occurrence.

---

## Three predictions, on the record

Let me be specific, because vague futurism is cheap.

1. The most consequential new software companies of the next three years will be started by one or two people.
Not because they couldn't hire, but because hiring would slow them down. The founder who can hold the whole picture moves faster than a team negotiating the picture across six Slack channels.

2. Vision becomes the bottleneck, not headcount.
The competitive advantage shifts from "how many engineers do you have" to "how clearly does your founder see the problem." The scarce resource is no longer talent. It's taste.

3. The infrastructure of modern business is about to be rebuilt from scratch.
The SaaS stack, the org chart, the fundraising ladder — all of it was designed for a world where building was hard and required armies. That world is disappearing. The infrastructure hasn't caught up yet. But it will.

And when it does, the people who figured this out early won't just have a head start. They'll be playing a different game entirely.

---

The future that everyone's debating? It's not approaching on the horizon. It's already in the rearview mirror. Most people just haven't looked yet.

This is the first thing I wanted on the record. More to come.
